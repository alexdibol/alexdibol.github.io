Algo Systems · Pillar I — Systems Discipline

Build the infrastructure that makes systematic work reviewable.

Pillar I is the foundation layer of Algo Systems. Its purpose is to establish methodological discipline before strategy exploration. It trains the engineering and governance posture that makes experiments reproducible, conclusions auditable, and claims defensible. Without this layer, backtests become stories; with it, research becomes reviewable evidence.

Pillar I is intentionally organized into two domains. Each domain is presented ladder-first: the first block is the core formation ladder (the primary entry point), followed by secondary modules that extend or specialize the ladder.

Two Domains, One Standard

The two domains reinforce each other while remaining conceptually distinct. AI Systems focuses on governed use of AI in financial workflows—evaluation posture, verification constraints, and controlled integration. Algorithmic Systems Engineering focuses on deterministic experimentation—backtesting infrastructure, execution realism, and artifact logging.

Both domains share the same institutional standard: explicit assumptions, reproducible runs, and stage gates before interpretation.

Domain I — AI Systems

This domain treats AI as a governed capability inside a broader system, not as an oracle. The focus is disciplined integration: evaluation, oversight, verification posture, and limits. The objective is to use AI to increase productivity and clarity without sacrificing auditability or epistemic control.

AI Systems Ladder

Core formation track
Primary Entry: The AI Systems Ladder

A structured progression for incorporating AI into systematic trading workflows under governance. The ladder is designed to prevent two common failures: (i) treating AI output as truth, and (ii) allowing AI convenience to erode reproducibility.

  • Evaluation posture: what AI can and cannot claim; how to separate drafting from verification
  • Governance constraints: disclosure risk, redaction discipline, review requirements, and accountability
  • Integration patterns: AI as tool for structure and productivity, never as decision authority
  • Auditability: prompt logging practices, versioning, and evidence trails supporting review

Domain II — Algorithmic Systems Engineering

This domain builds deterministic infrastructure: backtesting engines, execution realism, configuration control, and artifact logging. The objective is to make experimentation reviewable and conclusions defensible. This is the layer that prevents systematic research from degenerating into unrepeatable stories.

Algorithmic Systems Ladder

Core formation track
Primary Entry: The Algorithmic Systems Ladder

A structured progression for building the engineering substrate of systematic trading: deterministic backtests, execution and cost surfaces, reproducible configurations, and governance artifacts. The ladder is designed to ensure that every result can be re-run, audited, and challenged without ambiguity.

  • Deterministic backtesting: seeds, configuration control, and repeatable pipelines
  • Execution realism: costs, slippage, participation constraints, and convex impact sensitivity
  • Portfolio architecture: position sizing, constraints, and risk budgeting as explicit policy rules
  • Governance artifacts: run manifests, risk logs, evidence trails, and stage gates
